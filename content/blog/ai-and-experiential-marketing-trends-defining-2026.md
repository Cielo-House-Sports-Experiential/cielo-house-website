There is something happening in America right now that you genuinely have to experience in person to understand, and you can feel it the moment you step outside the airport.

The subway is plastered with ads for AI startups I had never heard of, the people on the train are actually arguing about which models write better, and I kept passing strangers walking down the street with their laptops open mid-prompt as though the footpath were just an extension of their desk. I sat next to a woman in a coffee shop in midtown who was running Claude on her phone the way most people text a friend, casually flicking between threads while she drank her coffee, and it struck me that this was not a trend or a moment, it was just the texture of daily life over there.

Two weeks of being immersed in it, first in New York and then at the Experiential Marketing Summit in Las Vegas, has genuinely reset my sense of how far along the conversation actually is, and the version of it we have been having back home in Australia is several chapters behind the version playing out on the ground in America.

![Woman using AI on her phone](/public/images/blog/ai-and-experiential-marketing-trends-defining-2026/image-01.jpg)

### The Shift in AI

The most important shift to understand is that the debate about whether to use AI is essentially over in America, and the conversation has moved on to the much sharper and more demanding question of how to use it with real skill, taste, and judgement.

The people I met are not asking themselves whether AI is going to change their work, they are asking which workflows to rebuild first, which subscriptions to cancel, which roles to reshape, and which pricing models to revise. The casualness is the part that should genuinely concern Australian businesses, because it is the sign of a market that has moved past novelty and into habit, and the businesses operating inside that market are now expected to be at the same level by the people they serve. When a CFO has been using AI in her personal life for six months, she does not need to be convinced of its value, she needs you to explain why your work has not already integrated it.

That shift becomes most concrete inside agencies, where I spoke with multiple founders who have rebuilt their internal operations from scratch, replacing five figure subscription software with custom-built tools and automating workflows that used to consume half a staff role. One spoke openly about the cultural shift he was driving inside his company to make it AI first, with every employee expected to use the tools and contribute to the prompt library that was rapidly becoming the agency's intellectual property. The competitive advantages being built right now are genuinely difficult to close in twelve months.

### Are we going the other way?

The most surprising part of the trip, though, was how strongly the smartest people in the room were pushing in the opposite direction at the same time.

Session after session at the Experiential Marketing Summit returned to the idea that as the world becomes more digitally saturated, the value of tactile, physical, in person moments is going up rather than down, and the brands paying attention to that shift are designing their experiences accordingly.

The panel on extending events into ongoing engagement spoke openly about audiences craving small, intimate moments rather than spectacle.

### Return on Emotion

Emotional resonance is now a measurable outcome and a stronger predictor of long term brand affinity than any impression count. The agency leaders forum used the word rawness to describe what clients are actually paying for, even as those same clients ask whether AI can do the work faster and cheaper.

The two themes, AI acceleration on one side and analog renaissance on the other, are not in tension at all, they are partners, and the agencies who understand that balance will be the ones leading the next decade.

![marketing agency team collaborating around a table with laptops and devices](/public/images/blog/ai-and-experiential-marketing-trends-defining-2026/image-03.jpg)

### ROI is a constant conversation

The theme I keep returning to is measurement, and specifically how much our industry is still struggling to defend its value to the people holding the budget. I sat in on a ROI roundtable session that was one of the most useful hours of the entire trip, and the honest reality was that almost nobody in the room had a clean answer for what would actually change if their events did not happen at all.

We talked about impressions, attendance, social reach, and pipeline attribution, and yet the simple question of counterfactual value made the conversation go quiet in a way that was genuinely revealing.

The brands and agencies winning that conversation are the ones who have done the harder work of defining behavioural change, tracking it over months and years rather than days, and being honest about which activities pass the test.

### Events as ecosystems

The shift from single events to ecosystems, and from sponsorships to genuine partnerships. Brand activations no longer exist as moments to be measured on the day, they exist as continuous journeys that begin weeks before the event with pre-event anticipation, run through the day itself with deeply considered participation design, and continue for months afterwards through content, follow-up, and community engagement.

Ford Racing's session on their 2026 motorsport launch made this concrete with their Rolling Thunder strategy, which used a series of interconnected activities to sustain momentum across the entire campaign rather than peaking on event day. The implication is that your measurement framework needs to span the entire journey rather than the eight hours at the venue, and your creative thinking needs to consider what happens on either side of the day with the same intent as the day itself.

### Where Australia is falling behind

Underneath every other conversation I had was the same uncomfortable observation, which is that the gap between businesses moving thoughtfully on all of this and businesses waiting to see what happens is widening faster than most people in Australia have registered.

The agencies investing now in AI fluency, measurement, ecosystem thinking, and genuine human craft are building a lead that will be very difficult for slower competitors to close in twelve months. The next year will define what good looks like in our industry for the rest of the decade, and the businesses that move with intent during that window will be the ones writing the rules everyone else follows.

I came home with a clear set of changes I am already making at Cielo, and the strong feeling that the energy in America right now mirrors what people describe from the early days of the internet, when something fundamentally new was being built in real time and the people paying close attention knew the ground had shifted under their feet. We have the chance to be those people right now, and the only thing standing between us and the front edge of that wave is the willingness to engage with it seriously, learn quickly, and build before we feel ready. I would rather be slightly early and slightly wrong about some of it than safely late and entirely irrelevant, and if there is one piece of advice I would give to every Australian business owner reading this, it would be exactly that.
